Colosseum

This time line will take you through the major events of the Colosseum through its life. Also the time line goes through some other events happening at the same time; around the world and in Italy. For those that don't know anything about the Colosseum, Its located in Rome Italy and as you'll learn in this time line is full of controversial history.

70 AD

Construction of the Colosseum began under the rule of Vespasian. Vespasian completed up to the third story by the time he died in 79. His son, Titus, finished the top level in year 80.

What else is happening?

The Roman Empire in 70 AD destroyed a temple in Jerusalem as a response to the Jewish community in Judea rebelling in 66 AD. The Romans killed thousands. The Romans took the treasure and gold and used that to build the Colosseum.

Replica Statue of Vespasian Located in the modern art museum in Moscow Russia

80 AD

The inaugural games were held in year 80. During the inaugural games Dio Cassius claims that over 9,000 wild animals were killed. Special coins were issued celebrating the inauguration

What else is happening?

Not much else is happening in the world at this moment, but the Roman empire is still growing and gladiator fights are still happening.

Pollice Verso

"With a turned thumb" is a painting by French artist Jean-Léon Gérôme. Painted in 1872

100 AD

The building was remodeled further under Vespasian's younger son, the newly designated Emperor Domitian. He Constructed the hypogeum; a series of underground tunnels used to house animals and slaves. He also added a gallery to the top of the Colosseum to increase the seating capacity.

What else is happening?

Not much is happening in the world at this time, but the Roman Army reaches 300,000 soldiers. In China, the wheelbarrow makes its first appearance and paper is now being used by the general populace.

Relief scene of Roman legionaries marching, from the Column of Marcus Aurelius, Rome, Italy, 2nd century AD

217 AD

During this time the Colosseum was badly damaged by a major fire started by lightning. The fire destroyed the wooden upper levels of the amphitheater's interior.

What else is happening?

Caracalla is assassinated by his soldiers near Edessa. Marcus Opellius Macrinus, head of the Praetorian Guard, declares himself Roman emperor. A Roman army, under the command of Macrinus, is defeated in a three days' battle by the Parthians at Nisibis, in the province of Mesopotamia.

Bust of Macrinus, from the Capitoline Museum

240 AD

The Colosseum was not fully repaired until 240 AD and underwent further repairs around 250 - 252 and more in 320 AD.

What else is happening?

Gladiator fights are starting to slow down and are last mentioned around year 435. There were also more restoration to parts of the Colosseum under Theodosius II and Valentinian II. The likely cause of the damage is a major earthquake in 433.

Ave Caesar! Morituri Te Salutant (Hail Caesar! We Who Are About to Die Salute You)

500 AD

The Colosseum underwent several radical changes of use. By the late 6th century a small chapel had been built into the structure of amphitheater. This did not confer any particular religious significance on the building as a whole.

What else is happening?

The arena was converted into a cemetery. The numerous vaulted spaces in the arcades under the seating were converted into housing and workshops and are recorded as still being rented out as late as the 12th century.

The Protestant Cemetery, Testaccio. Rome, Italy

1200 AD

The Frangipani family took over the Colosseum and fortified it; using it as a castle.

What else is happening?

Around this time there isn't much happening around this time in Rome and around the world.

The Colosseum as the Frangipani family castle.

1349 AD

An earthquake caused severe damage to the Colosseum. The outer south side is on less stable terrain and that caused the outer south side to collapse. A lot of the fallen stone was reused to build palaces, churches, hospitals and other buildings in Rome.

What else is happening?

Nothing good is happening in the world right now. Jewish populations are being killed because they were believed to be the cause of black death.

Citizens of Tournai bury plague victims

1365 AD

A religious order moved into the Colosseum in the mid-14th century and continued to inhabit it until as late as the early 19th century.

What else is happening?

There is not much recorded history here. There were some battles and the University of Vienna was founded

Map of medieval Rome depicting the Colosseum

1500 AD

During the 16th and 17th century, church officials sought a productive role for the Colosseum. Pope Sixtus V planned to turn the Colosseum into a wool factory. He also planned it would provide employment for Rome's prostitutes. This proposal fell through due to a premature death.

What else is happening?

In 1671 Cardinal Altieri authorized the use of the Colosseum for bullfights. A public outcry caused the idea to be hastily abandoned.

Pope Sixtus V or Xystus V (13 December 1521 – 27 August 1590), born Felice Piergentile, was Pope of the Catholic Church from 24 April 1585 to his death in 1590.

1807 AD

During this time popes initiated various stabilization and restoration projects. They removed overgrown vegetation because it was threatening to damage it further. The facade was reinforced with triangular brick wedges in 1807 and 1827.

What else is happening?

The interior was repaired in 1831, 1846 and in the 1930s. The arena substructure was partly excavated in 1810 - 1814 and 1874 and was fully exposed under Benito Mussolini in the 1930s.

The exposed substructure of the arena in 2013.

Today

The Colosseum is one of Rome's most popular tourist attractions, receiving millions of visitors annually.

The effects of pollution and general deterioration over time prompted a major restoration program carried out between 1993 and 2000, which cost $19.3 million USD (~$29m today)

What else is happening?

In recent years, the Colosseum has become a symbol of the international campaign against capital punishment, which was abolished in Italy in 1948.

Several anti–death penalty demonstrations took place in front of the Colosseum in 2000. Since that time, as a gesture against the death penalty, the local authorities of Rome change the color of the Colosseum's night time illumination from white to gold whenever a person condemned to the death penalty anywhere in the world gets their sentence commuted or is released, or if a jurisdiction abolishes the death penalty.

Most recently, the Colosseum was illuminated in gold in November 2012 following the abolishment of capital punishment in the American state of Connecticut in April 2012.
